Neurophysiology

Roles and Responsibilities

Neurophysiology is a job to diagnose people's neurological condition. For example epilepsy.

A Neurophysiologist works in many hospital settings such as:

  • General Wards
  • Operation Theatre
  • Emergency Departments
  • Community Settings
  • Intensive Care

On a typical day, a Neurophysiologist would conduct:

  • EEG (Electroencephalography) - This is conducting a recording of the electrical activity in the brain from the scalp. This is largely used to diagnose and monitor Epilepsy.
  • EMG (Electromyography) and NCS (Nerve Conduction Studies) - these assess the functioning of the nerves and muscles within the body. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome is commonly diagnosed using NCS. EMG is used in conditions affecting nerve and muscle function including myasthenia gravis and motor neuron disease.
  • Evoked Potentials - these are potentials produced by a brain in response to a certain stimulus for example a flashing light. These are used to diagnose various conditions including multiple Sclerosis.

There Are Many Different Ways to Join:

  • Apprenticeship: This is an entry level job and level 2 Apprenticeship. This requires 5 GCSEs at 9-4/A-C including maths, english and science.
  • Associate Practitioner: This is another route to a career in biomedical science. This requires a foundation degree or equivalent experience with training and experience to NVQ level 3.
  • Degree - Level Qualification: This is a level 6 Apprenticeship or degree course. This requires relevant level 3 Qualification for example 3 A levels with atleast one science subject or an access to higher education.
  • Post Graduate: This is the scientist training program. To start this you will need a 1st or 2:1 in relevant first degree or relevant higher degree and 2:2 in any subject. Higher specialist scientist training is available for registered clinical scientists or senior biomedical scientist with relevant MSc or specialist diploma.
